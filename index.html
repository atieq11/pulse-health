What the Color of Your Urine
Reveals About Your Health

Most Americans ignore one of the simplest — and most accurate — daily health signals their body sends. Doctors say checking urine color takes two seconds and can catch problems weeks before symptoms appear.

Pulse Health Editors
Medically reviewed · April 2026
4 min read

Your body sends hundreds of health signals every day — and most of them go completely unnoticed. But there is one signal so easy to check, so consistently informative, and so widely ignored that doctors have started calling it the most underused free health tool available to every American. The color of your urine changes based on your hydration, kidney function, liver health, diet, and even certain medications — and learning to read it takes about two seconds. According to the National Institutes of Health, urine color is one of the most reliable real-time indicators of how well your body is filtering waste, processing fluids, and maintaining internal balance.

"Urine color gives you a daily window into your hydration, kidney function, and liver health — and almost nobody pays attention to it."

The urine color guide — what each shade means

Pale straw / very light yellow
Ideal hydration. Your kidneys are filtering efficiently and you're well-hydrated.
Healthy
Bright / medium yellow
Normal and healthy. This is the most common color for well-hydrated adults.
Normal
Dark yellow / amber
Mild dehydration. Drink water now — your kidneys are conserving fluid.
Drink water
Orange / deep amber
Significant dehydration, or possibly a sign of liver or bile duct issues. Drink water and monitor closely.
Concern
Brown / cola-colored
Could indicate severe dehydration, muscle breakdown, or liver disease. See a doctor if this persists.
See a doctor
Cloudy or murky
May indicate a urinary tract infection (UTI), kidney stones, or excess protein. Worth a doctor visit.
See a doctor
Completely clear
Over-hydration. You're drinking more water than your kidneys need, which can dilute important electrolytes.
Slow down
Pink or red
Could be beets or certain foods — or blood in urine. If you haven't eaten red foods, see a doctor promptly.
Check first

A few important caveats most articles don't mention: certain foods and vitamins temporarily change urine color with no health significance. Beets and berries can turn urine pink or reddish. B vitamins (especially B2/riboflavin) turn urine bright neon yellow — completely harmless. Asparagus gives urine a distinctive odor within 30 minutes of eating it, due to a specific compound that the body metabolizes quickly. These changes resolve within a few hours and require no concern. The colors that warrant attention are persistent — meaning they appear consistently over multiple bathroom visits throughout the day, regardless of what you've eaten or how much you've drunk. If a concerning color persists for more than 24 hours, that's the signal to call your doctor.

What to remember

  • Pale to medium yellow = ideal. This is your daily target.
  • Dark yellow or amber = drink water now, you're mildly dehydrated.
  • Completely clear = slow down, you may be over-hydrating.
  • Brown, cloudy, or pink (without eating red foods) = see a doctor.
  • B vitamins make urine bright neon yellow — harmless and normal.
  • Check urine color mid-afternoon, not first thing in the morning when it's naturally concentrated.
